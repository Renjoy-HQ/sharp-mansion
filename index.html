On North Cascade Avenue — once home to fifty of Colorado's wealthiest families — the Sharp Mansion stands as the grandest survivor of an era that shaped a state. Designed in 1912 by master architect Nicholas van den Arend, its 14,840 square feet of Italian terracotta, gray brick, and African mahogany have witnessed more than a century of Colorado history.

Only four families have ever held the keys. Today, the Sharp Mansion opens its doors for private events, weddings, corporate gatherings, and luxury overnight stays — with every original detail intact, and every room ready to receive.

The Grand Mansion · Main Foyer
I.

The Grand Mansion

Nine bedrooms. Seven fireplaces. A century of flawless preservation.

Designed by Dutch-born architect Nicholas van den Arend — creator of the landmark Van Briggle Memorial Pottery — the mansion is a masterwork of Mediterranean arcades, Craftsman-beamed ceilings, and Jacobean ornament. At 14,840 square feet, it is among the largest and finest historic residences in the American West.

Every original Van Briggle ceramic tile fireplace remains intact across seven rooms. Original African Mahogany doors, preserved tapestry wallpaper in the dining room, and a working greenhouse off the main hall complete the picture. Nothing has been compromised. Nothing has been lost.

The Conservatory · Original 1912 Greenhouse
II.

The Conservatory

Glass, iron, and original tile — preserved exactly as built.

Accessible directly off the dining room, the conservatory is one of the most rare surviving features of any residential estate in Colorado. Its original tile floor, iron-framed glass ceiling, and Victorian proportions create a setting unlike anything that can be recreated or fabricated.

As a cocktail hour space, an intimate dinner setting, a ceremony backdrop, or a morning gathering for overnight guests — the conservatory transforms every occasion into something genuinely extraordinary.

The Grounds · Pergola at Dusk
III.

The Grounds

0.87 acres. A fountain. Vined pergola. The estate at night.

Enclosed by the original stone-and-iron perimeter fence, the grounds of Sharp Mansion are a destination in themselves. A columned pergola draped in climbing vines and strung with lights frames the central lawn. A sculptural fountain anchors the garden. A lighted pickleball court and putting green complete the picture.

At dusk, when the mansion's arched arcade glows amber and the pergola lights reflect in the fountain — there is no venue in Colorado Springs that compares. This is the setting that photographs themselves cannot fully prepare guests for.

The Carriage House · Private Guest Suite
IV.

The Carriage House

The original manservant's quarters — reimagined for the modern guest.

When Arthur Sharp built his estate in 1912, he built for a full household. The two-story carriage house — with its historic manservant's quarters — has been thoughtfully converted into a private luxury suite that sleeps up to eleven, while preserving the character and architectural detail of the original structure.

Three bedrooms including a king suite, a full kitchen, private entrance, hot tub access, and fast Wi-Fi. Available for overnight stays, as bridal party quarters, or as VIP guest accommodation for events held on the full estate.

The History

112 Years of
Colorado Legacy

1912

Arthur Sharp builds the finest home on the finest street in Colorado Springs.

In 1912, Arthur G. Sharp — president of the Exchange National Bank and steward of Colorado Springs' streetcar system since Winfield Scott Stratton's death in 1902 — commissioned architect Nicholas van den Arend to build a home equal to his standing in the city.

Van den Arend, a Dutch-born architect also responsible for the landmark Van Briggle Memorial Pottery, delivered a masterwork: 14,840 square feet of gray brick, local sandstone, and imported Italian terracotta tile, crowned by seven Van Briggle ceramic fireplaces that remain intact to this day.

1938

The man who ran Colorado Springs lived here for 25 years — and never left willingly.

Arthur Sharp moved his family to Colorado Springs from Burlingame, Kansas in 1895. He spent the next four decades shaping the city's financial and civic infrastructure as the 32-year president of the Exchange National Bank and president of the streetcar system.

He lived in his mansion until his death in 1938. His wife Louise remained. The home had never known another family, and every original detail — the fireplaces, the mahogany doors, the greenhouse — bore witness to their quarter-century of residence.

1942

El Pomar. The Red Cross. The United Way. The estate serves a city for 60 years.

In 1942, the El Pomar Foundation acquired the property and donated it to the American Red Cross, which made the Sharp Mansion its Colorado Springs chapter headquarters. For over six decades, the estate welcomed the city it had been built to preside over.

Through all of it — the Red Cross occupancy, the United Fund years, the United Way chapter — not one Van Briggle tile was disturbed. Not one mahogany door replaced. When the Red Cross departed in 2003, the mansion was left exactly as it had been built: intact, irreplaceable, and waiting.

Today

Four owners. 112 years. The estate is open — and it has never looked more ready.

The Sharp Mansion has had only four owners in its entire history. The current stewards have added a pickleball court, a putting green, and a pergola garden — while treating every original detail with the same reverence every previous owner showed. Thoughtfully. Carefully. And with the belief that what Arthur Sharp built deserves to endure.

Today the carriage house welcomes overnight guests. The conservatory hosts private dinners. The grounds accommodate events under Colorado skies. The Sharp Mansion is, at last, ready to share what it has always been — the finest private address in Colorado Springs.
